What are they?

Those who perform heavy physical labor are referred to as "blue-collar workers."
These workers are usually found in the manufacturing, construction, mining, agricultural, or maintenance industries.

Why are they called Blue-Collar Jobs

The phrase first appeared in the 1920s, when blue-collar workers were distinguished from white-collar office workers by donning darker-colored attire.
Probably because Blue-Collar workers got dirtier than white collar workers.

History of Blue-Collar Jobs

Blue-collar jobs have been essential for centuries, evolving from agricultural and artisanal work to modern industrial roles.
In the pre-industrial era, workers were mainly farmers and craftsmen. The Industrial Revolution shifted labor to factories, leading to urbanization and infrastructure growth.
The 20th century saw further expansion in industries like automotive and construction, with blue-collar workers driving mass production.
Though outsourcing and automation reduced traditional manufacturing jobs, new opportunities arose in fields like logistics and healthcare.
Today, blue-collar jobs remain crucial, adapting to technology while maintaining industries and infrastructure.
